Is Censorship Possible in the Technological Age?

Introduction

In the 20th century, during the zenith of totalitarian governments and state censorship, it was easy to control the flow of information. Through the censorship of oppositional views and the promotion of propaganda, states were able to create narratives that were virtually unopposed.

The most famous example of such mass-censorship in this era was the 1933 Nazi book burning, in which people took to the streets to cleanse all “un-German” literature from public record. Within a number of years the narrative woven by the Nazi elite would unfortunately prove Heinrich Heine’s prophetic statement correct,

“Where they burn books, they will also burn people.”

Book burning symbolises censorship in the purest of forms and while censorship would manifest itself again in such blatant forms, the motivation behind such campaigns would at least attempt to be concealed. Throughout the Cold War and the advancement of technology the power balance between censor and citizen appeared relatively stable, yet with the advent of the technological age a great shift occurred. A shift that hadn’t been felt since the creation of the medieval Gutenberg press.

The internet, much like the Gutenberg press, revolutionized the availability of information for the average person and broke the government’s monopoly on information. This new technology allowed instant access to information that challenged these narratives and upset the power balance between state and citizen.

In 2013, Edward Snowden, a former contractor for the CIA, leaked thousands of classified documents detailing the illegal mass surveillance of citizens within the United States. It’s been countered that this information was crucial to state security and thus secrecy was essential. Whether you view Snowden as a traitor or a freedom fighter is negligible, what is important is despite the labyrinthine apparatus of state censorship all of this information was leaked by a single individual using modern technology.

Yet with the dawn of this new information age the government’s censorship tools were not made obsolete, instead they were repurposed for the 21st century. So effectively repurposed that you mightn’t recognise censorship within your own country. Much like the arms race during the Cold War, we currently live in the newest iteration of the arms race between citizen and censor, between freedom and restriction. Governments have kept up to date with the latest censorship methods and some old methods linger that harken back to the dictatorial days of the 20th century.

In this essay I will be examining how censorship has and is being used in authoritarian governments, the ways in which people have fought against it and answer the question in point, is censorship still possible in the technological age?

The Soviet Union: Behind the Iron Curtain

I will use the Soviet Union as an exemplar of “traditional” means of censorship which I will later contrast with newer forms.

A few days after the Bolsheviks gained power in 1917 the first media censorship laws began. They offered the working class proletariat freedom with one hand and took it back with the other. This would be the first of many censorship laws that would be passed, each one more extreme than the last.

State censorship was regulated by the Glavlit which was the governmental censorship and state secret protection department. By 1930 all printed material was examined and then censored by them before publication. Their overzealous tactics were created to strike fear into the populace, Professor of Modern European History, Roger D. Markwick states, they used“a draconian system of censorship coupled with lethal repression.” They expanded their censorship laws to all elements of Communist society. Everything from art to music was moulded to fit Soviet doctrine restraining artists to a select few set of themes.

With the end of World War Two the Soviet Union pursued an isolationist policy and as Churchill stated in his famous speech

“an iron curtain [had] descended across the continent.”

This effectively blocked outside information from entering the Eastern Bloc allowing the State to be the sole source of information and their narrative as paramount to truth. Even the past was re-written to fit state propaganda. Markwick postulates this revision of history was used to “maintain a hegemonic narrative about the past that vindicates the status quo.”

Many state opponents, or nonpersons, were sentenced to harsh punishments. Such as murdered in the Great Purges, left to die in a Siberian gulag or whitewashed from history completely. All visual or historical reference to them would be destroyed in an effort to falsify history. Author of The Commissar Vanishes, David King writes Stalin whitewashed his political rivals in order to portray himself as

"successor to Lenin, the leader of the Bolshevik Revolution and founder of the USSR."

Similarly many citizens rushed to “purge” convicted family members from photographs as a way to avoid drawing suspicion or a similar fate. Much of the populace pursued this “self-censorship” attitude as a means of self-preservation.

Despite the threat of violence, the regime had its detractors. A grassroots network, Samizdat (self-published), spread hand copied banned material throughout the Eastern Bloc. Soviet Dissenter, Vladimir Bukovskij describes the reality of being a member of the Samizdat, "I myself create it, edit it, censor it, publish it, distribute it, and [may] get imprisoned for it." Due to the State owning all printing presses each book had to be hand copied.

Natalya Gorbanevskaya was founder of the most famous Samizdat periodical, A Chronicle of Current Events. Author and media expert Peter Kirwan writes,

“…Gorbanebskaya would tap out six identical copies of Chronicle on a contraband typewriter. Next, she distributed these editions to six friends, who would, in turn, type out further copies before distributing them to additional readers.”

Dissemination of this information was dangerous and much care had to be taken to avoid detection. According to writer Benjamin Ramm, Exiled Soviet poet Osip Mandelstam had his poems “sewn into the insides of cushions and shoes, or hidden in mattresses and saucepans.”

It is also through such clandestine methods that banned materials could pass through the Iron Curtain. A similar practice called Tamizdat (published over there) would use more modern approaches such as rock and roll music or political tracts smuggled in on bootleg records made from x-ray film. In the Socialist Republic of Romania under Nicolae Ceaușescu, many western films were smuggled into the Eastern Bloc and within a day of the tapes entering Romania they were translated, copied and distributed. The bootlegged videos were immensely popular and a huge black market formed.

In the documentary Chuck Norris vs. Communism, a subject described watching these bootlegged videos as “a realization of how far behind the West and how culturally isolated their Eastern European nation had become.”The man behind this bootlegging operation, Teodor Zamfir, states in the same documentary,

“During a dictatorship which had controlled everything, they lost control of something that seemed insignificant, the videotape. The videotapes set the whole communist system off balance."

Other outside influences such as Radio Free Europe also penetrated the Iron Curtain and worked to undermine and challenge the Soviet narrative. This lead to the government trying to jam radio signals. Lithuanian radio journalist, Rimantas Pleikis, stated in a Russia Beyond article

“the USSR possessed the most powerful and wide scale “anti-radio” system in the world.”

This jamming campaign was viewed more as a nuisance and paradoxically made these foreign narratives even more popular. Citizens bypassed these censorship methods in creative manners. Kritika Mikkonen states in his essay Stealing the Monopoly of Knowledge, some methods involved

“…readjusting [radio] sets with bits collected here….there were cases where even junior school children started to build their own sets in order to listen to Western broadcasts.”

By the late 1980s over 60 foreign radio stations were broadcasting into the Eastern Bloc which forced the futile jamming efforts to cease entirely.

During the later years of the Soviet Union, political reforms such as glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring) allowed more freedom of expression and exposed much of the tension harboured after years of repression. With this new openness, the Soviet population noticed the abundance of products available in capitalist societies (particularly America) compared to the relative scarcity in the Eastern Bloc. This clash of realities often dubbed the war between the refrigerator and the television, or peoples living conditions vs state propaganda, was a serious issue for the State. It severely undermined the propaganda of the time and cracks in the state narrative became obvious.

On November 9, 1989 the Berlin Wall fell, the Iron curtain was drawn and a period of rapid change galvanized the many nations and its peoples to action. Many Soviet writers reflected on the past years of repression that followed, succinctly called Omni-censorship (ВСЕЦЕНЗУРА) by Russian scholar Marianna Tax Choldin. Bibliographer for Slavic, East European and Eurasian Studies, June Pachuta Farris, states the realities of living under the Soviet yoke, censorship was

“…all-pervasive, and woven through the entire fabric of Soviet society. All citizens…knew not only what they MUST NOT do, but also what they MUST do in order to get ahead or in the worst of times, just to survive. If unexpressed thoughts could have been censored, they would have been…”

The Soviet model of censorship used “traditional” blunt tools, censorship was obvious and punishments were often brutal. The government struggled with competing narratives and factions, from within and without, which ultimately lead to its dissolution. Even though they forced the populace into a terminal state of self-censorship they ultimately lost when the illusions they painted simply couldn’t compete with reality.

China: The Great Firewall of China

Unlike the Soviets, China’s censorship tools became more covert as they evolved. While the censorship methods were just as brutal to the Soviet Union’s during the time of China’s Cultural Revolution they changed with technological progress.

China possesses the largest censorship and surveillance program in the world. With an estimated 2 million online censors being employed in China, according to a study in 2013 by the BBC. The People’s Republic of China also has the dubious honour of having

"the largest recorded number of imprisoned journalists and cyber-dissidents in the world,"

according to the Ashgate Research Companion to Chinese Foreign Policy."

According to Senior Fellow of Asian Studies, Gudrun Wacker, The PRC will censor any information that, “[...] endangers national security, discloses state secrets, subverts the government, destroys the unity of the country. [...] Damages the honour and the interests of the State. [...] disturbs social order, under -mines social stability [...]”

While this statute seems reasonable the language used is deliberately undefined allowing a wider form of censorship and persecution where needed. The ability for the government to manipulate such laws enforces a stricter personal and corporate self-censorship attitude. The responsibility of enforcing these laws on the behalf of the government sometimes falls on international or private companies and so they are overzealous in their censorship enforcement in order to avoid financial punishments or criminal proceedings.

The famed Great Firewall of China is the jewel in the crown of the countries censorship apparatus. In simple terms it controls the flow of information in and out of China. It does this through a number of known technical methods such as banning websites than possess certain sensitive keywords, corrupting web addresses, thus making the site inaccessible and appointing human censors to manually analyse information. This state apparatus is constantly being improved with hopes of Artificial Intelligence (A.I.) being implemented in the future.

The banning of sites often seems to happen randomly, almost as a method of confusing netizens. The purpose of this censorship methodology is not to ban all external information, but to filter what passes through the membrane of the countries internet borders. This form of “porous censorship”, as Roberts puts it, partially filters out external narratives as it

“allows the government to avoid widespread use of observable repression.”

This methodology is further used when censoring internal subversive materials. Often websites that post scathing reviews about the government are not likely to be censored. However websites focusing on Tibetan Independence, political reforms or Party corruption certainly will. This is reinforced by a Harvard study by King who postulates, China’s censorship program

“is not to suppress criticism of the state” but to “reduce the probability of collective action by clipping social ties whenever any collective movements are in evidence."

On rare occasions when a journalist or cyber dissident posts a story that the Party deem unacceptable there is often a high level public trial. This has a chilling effect on public discourse and acts as a deterrent for netizens activities. This methodology is sometimes dubbed Kill the chicken to scare the monkeys in China. It is through this strategy of constant surveillance, optional self-censorship and rare but harsh punishments that makes porous censorship so effective.

Self-censorship, ironically, is the most effective of all of China’s censorship tools. Their censorship program has been labelled a

“panopticon that encourages self-censorship through the perception that users are being watched, rather than a true firewall” according to a study by UC Davis.

According to the Oxford Dictionary, a panopticon is a “circular prison with cells arranged around a central well, from which prisoners could at all times be observed.”

However in social theory panopticonism is a way to control the behaviour of people through the “perception” of constant surveillance and palpable presence of power. Even though constant surveillance and demonstration of power is not possible as long the illusion persists, the prisoner will act accordingly.

Due to this constant threat, the PRC effectively controls the actions of its citizens. This form of self-censorship extends further into social scenarios in real life, whether talking in a public forum or discussing sensitive subjects. In the documentary China on China, writer, Yan Lianke, says

“China's censorship is not as rigorous as everyone thinks. The self-censorship of the authors is much worse.”

Despite this, it is possible for netizens to circumvent these censorship methods. Technical methods include using proxy servers or VPNs to access banned websites. In response to their use many VPNs have been compromised and rumours of proxy servers being used as “honey traps” by the government to gather details of users have curtailed their use somewhat. There is no official proof of these honey traps however it fits in with the panopticon concept of an imagined threat.

Other technical methods of evasion include encryption of messages, peer-to-peer software or changing IP addresses in case the government has blocked a user. Every time a new method of avoiding censorship appears, the government has a counter-measure to negate it. Here we see the technological arms race between censor and citizen play out in real time.

Many netizens have found creative ways of bypassing the Great Firewall through the use of linguistic methods and satire. This allows the user to discuss off limit topics without fear of censorship or punishment.

Perhaps the most famous example is referring to internet censors as “river crabs” (河蟹). River crab is a close homophone of “harmony” (和諧), which in this case is a joke referring, to former president Hu Jintao’s concept of a “Harmonious Society”. This term, "Harmonious Society", has emerged as a byword for suppression, censorship or "stability at all costs". Thus when a netizens website has been censored, they can say their work has been "harmonized" (被和谐了). Over time "harmonized" changed into “river-crabbed” as government censors were learning of its euphemistic usage. We see once again evidence of a censorship arms-race, with each side trying to outpace the other.

Author of The Chinese Language as a Weapon, Jeanette Si writes

“On today’s Chinese Internet, fully comprehending the language requires a thorough knowledge of current events, a deep respect for historical implications, an agile mastery of cultural conventions, and more often than not, a healthy appreciation of topical humor.”

Although these methods are detected by human censors, these words are so commonly used that censoring would be catastrophic for China’s internet services. This highlights the reality of pan-censorship in the technological age. Former State Internet Information Director, Lu Wei states in Margaret Robert’s book, Censored,

“It is not possible to apply censorship to this enormous amount of data. But no censorship does not mean no management.”

This is the best way of looking at censorship in China; the State simply tries to manage the flow of information as it’s ultimately futile to censor all subversive narratives.

China is the embodiment of censorship in the 21st century, while they lack the brutalism of the Soviet system they make up for it through technical proficiency and stealthy methodologies. Visible widespread repression is reduced and the tools of censorship are sharpened to cut social ties and collective action. Through pervasive surveillance they have birthed a culture of self-censorship. Yet this entire technical system can be defied through the all too human custom of linguistic humour and satire.

North Korea: The Hermit Kingdom

Censorship in the Hermit Kingdom is a blend of old Soviet and new Sino methods that have been magnified into sheer totalitarianism. Everything within North Korea is geared towards isolationism and censorship. Due to this it continues to place last in the World Press Freedom Index, with Reporters without Borders adding “its totalitarian regime keeps its citizens in a state of ignorance.”

Ever since the creation of the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea in 1948, its ruler and founder Kim Il-sung ruled through suppression, fear and ruthless punishment. There was a wide campaign of censorship and propaganda, deifying Kim Il-sung and spreading his unique form of Juche (self-reliance) Communism. This form of strict censorship, limiting of basic human rights and isolationist policy has continued to this very day.

The North Korean State has authoritarian control of all forms of media and communication within its borders. Due to this monopoly they can control the narrative of all outside events and change public opinion to suit the Party’s interests.

Despite the North Korean constitution guaranteeing freedom of speech (Article 67) any reporting that is not sanctioned by the government is illegal and punishable. Reporters are re-educated in Party doctrine weekly and are closer to political activists. Vincent Brossel from Reporters without Borders states in his fact-finding report, “There are four categories of reporting. The priority is publicising the greatness of Kim Il-sung and his son Kim Jong-il. Second place goes to demonstrating the superiority of North Korean socialism. Third is denouncing imperialist and bourgeois corruption."

Any form of State criticism is considered treason however even minor infractions carry a heavy sentence. Vincent Brossel interviewed an anonymous North Korean escapee who stated,

“I was punished for six months, forced to work on a pig farm, for forgetting to write the last syllable of Kim Jong-il.”

Self-censorship extends down to the North Korean populace, more as form of self-preservation. In 2016, according to Radio Free Asia, “Kim Jong-un banned sarcasm because he fears people only agree with him ironically.” Such phrases as “this is all America’s fault” and reference to him being “a fool who cannot see the outside world” carry the ominous threat that the offender will “not be forgiven.”

Public criticism of the State is dangerous due to the vast network of undercover informants within the country. There primary role is to monitor and report on any individuals that don’t comply with Party protocol or possess any foreign material. Nicholas Eberstadt, a North Korea expert at the American Enterprise Institute says,

"It's as close to a Big Brother society as a society populated by human beings can possibly be. There are multiple competing secret police who spy on the people and on each other. So, no one can relax and have a private life."

This mass surveillance has been furthered by the installation of microphones into outdoor areas and extensive wiretapping of phones. So North Korean citizens are not truly free in the real world, well what about the bastion of freedom that is the internet?

Kwangmyong, the most tightly controlled and monitored state intranet in the world, possesses a mere 1000-5000 sites with only high ranking officials having limited access to the world internet through a single fibre optic line in and out of the country. All computers are registered with the government, systematically checked and heavily monitored. The financial cost of owning a computer equates to “as much as three months average salary” for a normal citizen, according to a Telegraph report by Mike Wright. Thus the far reaching liberating effects of the internet are stifled completely. North Korea has been described, by Julien Pain of Reporters without Borders, as “the world’s worst Internet black hole” and it appears to be by design.

Professor of International Law at Harvard, Jonathan Zittrain, states in a NY Times interview that access to the internet would conflict with its “comprehensive official fantasy worldview” and “any information leakage from the outside world could be devastating”.

Another reason for this impenetrable barrier is to make it

“extremely difficult for people in North Korea to tell the outside world about systematic, widespread and gross human rights violations...”

according to a fact-finding report from Amnesty international called Connection Denied.

Despite the isolationism of North Korea, television and radio waves still manage to penetrate its borders. The radio sets sold in North Korea are pre-set to government frequencies and are sealed to prevent any form of tampering. There are Party operatives, named the inminban, within each province that regularly check televisions and radios for any form of tampering or illegal usage. Despite this it is possible to obtain black market radios that allow citizens to listen to external radio media. Much like the Soviets, the DPRK attempt to jam these radio and television signals however they are usually ineffective due to routine power cuts.

Usually threats of excessive violence are enough to deter the majority of the populace. Yet some brave citizens have risked punishment to smuggle in mobile phones, DVDs, USBs or foreign material. Just like in Socialist Romania, there is a vast want for foreign media from everyday citizenry and higher party officials. According to a report called A Quiet Opening, by Nat Kretchun and Jane Kim,

“one third of television viewers [are] claiming to have viewed foreign content.”

In the same report an anonymous North Korean female said “almost all citizens listen or watch. You can tell when you talk to them…they will use [South] Korean words… you think, “that person watches too.””

The cultural impact of foreign media is starting to manifest in different ways. Higher party officials are buying more imported western goods, western clothes and styling their hair in ways not preordained by the government.

Perhaps the most effective method of escaping censorship in North Korea is the use of mobile phones. Despite them being introduced and then banned by the government, they are more popular than ever. They were originally used by traders in order to source food from China in the nineties during the worst times of the countries famine.

Other than allowing foreign information into the country they also allow the populace to spread information about the Hermit Kingdom to the outside world. Some brave individuals contact outside journalists, particularly in South Korea, and provide them with images or messages about the conditions within the country. Some even act as self-publishing journalists by spreading subversive information to other citizens within North Korea.

The consequence of contacting any South Korean is the infamous three generations of punishment, in which not only is the person punished, but so is their family. Then the next two generations of the family born in these labour camps are also sentenced. According to a Telegraph article by Mike Wright,

“Survivors have described prisoners becoming stunted and deformed from carrying out hard labour for 12 hours a day, seven days a week.”

And yet people still defy the State in the pursuit of truth.

North Korea’s form of isolationism and censorship borders on the absurd. The State segregates itself from the outside world entirely, leaving the populace in a state of ignorance and at the whims of a self-proclaimed quasi-deity. Surveillance is perpetual and true freedom of expression is non-existent. Traditional dictatorial repression and brutal punishment serve as deterrents, yet a large minority still risk their lives in pursuit of freedom and truth despite the consequences.

Conclusion

Many states have had to change their censorship practices with the dawn of the technological age. Soviet Russia relied on “traditional” terror, punishment and propaganda to enforce its political ideology which ultimately failed. China “sells” censorship in a market they control and bans efforts at group collectivism, birthing a culture of self-censorship. North Korea uses a combination of traditional and modern censorship methods combined with sheer isolationism and brutal repression.

They all try to create this thin veneer of freedom and impartiality yet under the surface the mechanisms of censorship are at work. I would argue that the old authoritarian and dictatorial spirit is still within these governments actions and yet they must hide these from the populace for fear of the repercussions. Some of the governments examined use moral arguments to justify their actions; others use economic or nationalistic reasons. Yet they all have political motivations to justify the status quo within each state.

Despite these states’ crafted narratives and various censorship methods, the ceaseless flow of information is unstoppable. The illusions that are painted by these narratives never stand the test of time; sooner or later reality always comes crashing in. The perfect panopticon is still nothing but a philosophical concept with no true physical embodiment. Yet with the ever increasing reach of technology I can’t help but feel we are edging towards this as a possibility.

Technology might be the antidote and the poison depending on who wields this powerful tool. As Professor Philip Bennett states in Columbia Journalism Review article,

“Technological innovation will…enable individuals and organizations to counteract government censorship, even as governments adopt technologies that enhance their ability to censor.”

It is a constant arms race between two forces. I would argue that technology is merely the tool, the true battle between censorship and freedom is between the will of the people versus the governments will to control.

Yes censorship is possible in the technological age, for a time, but it waxes and wanes with the constant power struggle between citizen and censor.
